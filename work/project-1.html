TRIOS 5: Product launch and updating pages

3Shape
Client
Service
UI Design
I had the rewarding opportunity to serve as a UI designer for a high-priority project over several months at our company. The task at hand was a critical one: design and launch new pages on our corporate website for our newest innovation, the TRIOS 5 scanner, while simultaneously updating existing pages for TRIOS 3 and TRIOS 4 products.
Building on our history of crafting world-class products, the new pages needed to encapsulate the excellence of the TRIOS series, which had won multiple Red Dot awards. The TRIOS 5, as the pinnacle of our scanner line, demanded a representation that could mirror its high-quality performance and innovative features. It was crucial to reflect not only the functional brilliance of the TRIOS 5 but also its status as a symbol of our commitment to providing cutting-edge, award-winning digital dentistry solutions. We had to ensure the digital representation did justice to the product that sets industry standards.

Intelligent. Effortless. Ergonomic

‍Our objective was clear: to create a visually engaging and compelling narrative around the TRIOS scanner product line, particularly focusing on our latest model, TRIOS 5. We were challenged to accomplish this within the constraints of our CMS, Sitecore, and in seven different languages to ensure our global customer base was catered to. Despite our ambitions, we had to reassess our approach due to the absence of proper prioritization, which led us to descope some new components while updating existing ones.

The Design Process

Once the content team provided me with the necessary copy and strategy, I was eager to get started. However, it was a bit of a challenge at first. Traditionally, our company has followed a Design-First approach. This means designers first create the visual mockups, which then serve as a foundation for the content team to build a compelling story around. In this scenario, though, we realized that strategy wouldn't quite fit our needs. So, we pivoted to a Content-First approach instead. Collaborating closely with the content team, we were able to construct a more meaningful narrative for the pages at hand. With this solid foundation in place, I then set out to bring these ideas to life through design.
Once the designs were finalized, we conducted a swift test to verify that our concept was clear. Then, it was back to the content team for a final round of revisions. Their task was to refine the copy once more, ensuring our primary goal — telling a compelling story would be successfully achieved.
